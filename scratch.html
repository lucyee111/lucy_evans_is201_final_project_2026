A Brief History of Film Photography

Film photography traces its roots to the early 19th century, when inventors like Louis Daguerre and William Henry Fox Talbot were racing to fix light onto a permanent surface. The daguerreotype, introduced in 1839, was the first widely used photographic process — a single, mirror-like image on a silver-coated copper plate. Beautiful, fragile, and completely unrepeatable.

The introduction of flexible roll film by George Eastman in the 1880s democratized photography. His Kodak camera came preloaded with film and could be sent back to the factory for development — "You press the button, we do the rest." Photography was no longer just for professionals with heavy glass plates and portable darkrooms.

Through most of the 20th century, film was photography. The mid-century golden age gave us the 35mm SLR, the Leica rangefinder, Kodachrome slide film, and iconic photojournalism from names like Henri Cartier-Bresson and Dorothea Lange. Then digital arrived in the late 1990s, and within a decade, film sales collapsed.

But something strange happened: film didn't die. It stubbornly persisted — and in the last five years, it has genuinely grown. Kodak has re-introduced discontinued film stocks. Film camera prices on eBay have surged. Darkrooms at universities are full again. Something about analog resonates with people who grew up in a world of instant, infinite, disposable images.

Types of Film Cameras

The world of film cameras is wonderfully varied. I shoot with three — each one feels totally different in your hands and produces a different result. Here's a breakdown of the main camera families, including the ones I own:

  1. 35mm Point-and-Shoot — I shoot: Olympus Infinity & Olympus IS-20 DLX
    • Compact and lightweight — easy to throw in a bag and forget about until the moment matters
    • Automatic exposure means you focus on the scene, not the settings
    • The Olympus Infinity is my most-reached-for camera — simple, reliable, and produces beautiful results with Gold 200
    • The IS-20 DLX adds a zoom lens, which is great for getting closer without moving your feet
    • Perfect entry point into film — approachable without sacrificing image quality
  2. 35mm SLR (Single Lens Reflex) — I shoot: Nikon FE
    • Full manual control — aperture, shutter speed, and focus are all in your hands
    • Through-the-lens viewfinder: you see exactly what the lens captures
    • The Nikon FE is a classic from 1978 — built like a tank, smooth to shoot, and pairs beautifully with Nikkor lenses
    • Interchangeable lenses open up a whole world of creative possibilities
    • Forces you to understand exposure properly — the best way to grow as a photographer
  3. Rangefinder
    • Focusing via a coincident image in a separate viewfinder — different feel from SLRs
    • Generally quieter and more discreet, beloved by street photographers
    • The Leica M series is the pinnacle; the Voigtländer Bessa is a more accessible entry point
    • On my list for someday — the shooting experience is completely unique
  4. Medium Format
    • Larger negative (typically 6×6cm or 6×7cm) = dramatically more detail and richer grain
    • Usually 10–16 frames per roll — forces even more intentionality per shot
    • The image quality jump over 35mm is immediately visible, especially in portraits
    • Cameras like the Hasselblad 500C/M and Mamiya RB67 are icons of the format
  5. Large Format (4×5 and 8×10)
    • Sheet film loaded one frame at a time — the ultimate in deliberate photography
    • Perspective control via tilt and shift movements unavailable on any other format
    • Can take an hour to set up and compose a single frame
    • Ansel Adams shot almost exclusively large format — those prints are why

My Film Stock

Ask most film photographers what's in their bag and you'll get a long, opinionated answer. Mine is short: Kodak Gold 200, almost exclusively. Here's why — and a couple of others worth knowing about.

Kodak Gold 200 ★ My Go-To

Warm, slightly soft, with a nostalgic quality that feels true to how I actually remember moments. It handles bright Utah sunlight beautifully, and the skin tones are some of the most natural I've seen from any film. Most of the photos on this page were shot on Gold 200. It's inexpensive, widely available, and consistently gives me exactly what I'm looking for.

Kodak Portra 400

When I need more speed — shooting in shade, indoors, or during golden hour — Portra 400 is the upgrade. Finer grain than Gold, with those famous creamy skin tones. A lot of photographers shoot this exclusively; I reach for it when conditions call for it.

Ilford HP5 Plus

My black-and-white choice when I want something different. Incredibly versatile — shoot it at box speed for clean results or push it to 1600 for something grainier and moodier. There's something timeless about a black-and-white film shot that color can't replicate.

The Development Process

One of the most common questions I get from people who are curious about film: "What happens after you shoot the roll?" The answer is wonderfully chemical.

Color Negative (C-41)

Most color film uses the C-41 process, a standardized chemical process that nearly every photo lab in the world can run. You drop off your roll, and a lab runs it through a series of chemical baths: developer, bleach, fix, stabilizer. It takes about 30 minutes of machine time. This is what you use for Portra, Gold, Ektar, and most consumer color films.

Black & White

B&W film is the one you can process yourself at home without a darkroom. All you need is a changing bag (to load film in total darkness), a daylight development tank, and three chemicals: developer, stop bath, and fixer. The choice of developer dramatically changes the look — Kodak D-76 for classic tonality, Rodinal for sharp grain, Caffenol (yes, coffee-based) for those who want to go fully DIY.

Since I shoot Kodak Gold 200 — a C-41 film — I send my rolls to a lab rather than developing at home. There's something I actually love about that: you drop off the roll, and a few days later you get your scans back. The wait is part of the experience. I've never been disappointed opening a folder of Gold 200 scans.

Slide Film (E-6)

Color reversal film — what you see is a positive image on the film itself, not a negative. The E-6 process is more complex and temperature-sensitive than C-41, which is why it's becoming harder to find labs that offer it. But the results are stunning: rich, saturated colors with much higher contrast than negative film. Kodachrome, the most famous slide film ever made, used a proprietary process so complex that it was discontinued in 2010 when the last processing lab closed.

A great overview from B&H Photo covering everything you need to know to start shooting film:

Why Film in the Digital Age?

I got into film photography almost by accident. I found an Olympus Infinity at a thrift store for a few dollars, bought a roll of Kodak Gold 200 on a whim, and took it on a road trip through the Tetons. When the scans came back a week later, I was completely hooked. There was something about those images — the warmth, the slight softness, the feeling that they looked like memories rather than documentation — that my phone camera had never produced.

I've been shooting film ever since. Here's what keeps me coming back:

The constraint is the point. With digital, the cost of a bad photo is essentially zero. You fire off 500 shots and keep 12. With film, each frame on a roll of 36 costs about $0.50 once you factor in film and development — so you think before you shoot. That constraint makes you a more careful, deliberate photographer. It's like writing a letter by hand versus firing off a text.

The wait changes how you see. There's usually a gap of days or weeks between when I shoot a roll and when I get the scans back. Looking at your own images with some distance is a completely different experience than swiping through shots on your camera screen two minutes after taking them. You see them more clearly. You're more objective.

The look is genuinely different. Grain, halation, color rendering, dynamic range characteristics — film has an aesthetic that you can try to replicate in post-processing, but never quite nail. Part of it is the optics (many vintage lenses have aberrations that give images character), and part of it is the chemical nature of the medium itself.

It's a physical artifact. When you shoot digital, your photos live on a hard drive or in the cloud. When you shoot film, you have a physical negative — a strip of plastic with your exact moment encoded in silver halide crystals. That negative exists independently of any technology or platform. It can outlive every digital format we use today. That means something to me.
